# How to Price AI Services

Pricing AI services is difficult because the underlying costs (LLM inference, hosting, human review) do not map intuitively to buyer value, and because many AI capabilities are new enough that market rates are still forming.

This guide covers a practical approach to setting initial prices, testing them, and adjusting based on evidence.

## Start with the buyer's value, not your costs

The most common pricing mistake in AI services is pricing based on delivery complexity or tool cost. Buyers do not pay for your effort or your API bills. They pay to solve a problem or achieve an outcome.

Before setting a price, answer:
- What problem does this solve for the buyer?
- What would it cost them to solve this problem without your service?
- What is the value of solving this problem (time saved, cost avoided, revenue enabled)?
- What would a reasonable buyer pay for a 10:1 return on investment?

A 10:1 value-to-price ratio is often cited as a useful starting heuristic: if the buyer gains $10,000 in value, pricing at $1,000 is likely acceptable. Pricing is a hypothesis and must be tested.

## Model your costs before setting prices

Before setting a price, calculate your cost floor:
- LLM/API cost per delivery unit
- Infrastructure and hosting cost
- Human review and editing time
- Client communication time
- Revision and support time
- Tools and software subscriptions allocated per delivery

Your price must exceed your costs with enough margin to sustain the business. For service businesses, 50–70% gross margin is a common target.

## Set a pricing hypothesis and test it

Do not spend weeks modeling the perfect price. Set a hypothesis, sell to the first few buyers, and adjust based on feedback:
- High conversion without price objections may mean you are underpriced
- Consistent price objections require investigation: is it the price, the value, or the buyer fit?
- Zero interest despite outreach may indicate a positioning or targeting problem, not a price problem

## Avoid these common mispricing patterns

- Pricing by the hour — this removes the value of AI efficiency gains
- Pricing too low to avoid rejection — low prices signal low quality and make you unprofitable
- Adding "AI" as a justification for a price premium without demonstrating the value
- Not accounting for support and revision time in fixed-price offers

## Use anchoring when presenting prices

Anchor pricing by showing what alternatives cost:
- What does a human agency charge for the same output?
- What does the buyer currently spend on a workaround solution?
- What is the cost of the problem if it goes unsolved for another 12 months?

Anchoring helps buyers calibrate the price relative to context rather than in isolation.

## Document your pricing rationale

Write down the reasoning behind each price point, including:
- The value delivered to the buyer
- The cost floor
- Market reference points
- The assumptions you are testing

This documentation helps you explain the price confidently and helps you learn from buyer feedback.
