# Value-Based Pricing

Value-based pricing sets a price based on the value the buyer receives, not on the cost of delivery or competitive benchmarks. It is the most effective pricing approach for AI services because the same AI capability can deliver vastly different value to different buyers.

## How value-based pricing works

The starting point is the buyer's outcome:

1. Identify the specific outcome the buyer cares about
2. Quantify that outcome: time saved, cost reduced, revenue generated, risk reduced
3. Calculate the annual or lifetime value of that outcome
4. Price at a fraction of that value (typically 10–20%)

If an AI workflow saves a marketing team 20 hours per month at an internal cost of $80/hour, the value delivered is $1,600/month. A price of $800–$1,200/month captures significant value for the buyer while returning strong revenue for the provider.

## When to use value-based pricing

Value-based pricing works best when:
- The buyer's value is quantifiable and meaningful
- You understand the buyer's cost structure well enough to anchor the value conversation
- The buyer is making a rational business decision, not an emotional purchase
- You can demonstrate the value with evidence (pilot, case study, reference)

## Discovery questions for value-based pricing

Before quoting a price, understand the buyer's value:
- How much time does this problem currently consume?
- What does that time cost in salary or opportunity cost?
- What is the consequence of not solving this problem for another 12 months?
- What have you tried before, and what did that cost?
- What would a successful outcome be worth to you?

These questions help you understand the value and help the buyer articulate it to themselves, which strengthens the case for your price.

## Anchoring the value conversation

Present value before price. If you discuss the price before the buyer understands the value, the price has no context. Walk through the outcome and the value calculation first, then introduce the price as a fraction of that value.

## Limitations of value-based pricing

- Requires detailed discovery before pricing — harder to package
- Some buyers resist sharing financial information needed to calculate value
- Value is hard to measure for some outcomes (brand, confidence, risk reduction)
- Value-based pricing can be challenged when the buyer's situation changes

Pricing is a hypothesis. Value calculations are estimates that must be tested and adjusted with real buyer feedback.
