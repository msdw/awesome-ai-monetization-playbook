# Pricing Objection Handlers

Price objections are part of every sales process. This guide covers common objections and honest, non-manipulative responses.

The goal is not to overcome objections through persuasion tactics. The goal is to understand the objection, respond honestly, and either resolve a genuine misunderstanding or accept that the fit is not right.

## Template

```
PRICING OBJECTION REFERENCE GUIDE
===================================

---

## Objection: "That's more than I expected."

This is the most common pricing objection. It may mean:
- The buyer did not understand the scope before seeing the price
- The buyer has a lower budget than the price requires
- The price is genuinely higher than alternatives they are considering

Response options:

1. Clarify scope:
   "I want to make sure the scope is clear. What was the scope you were 
   expecting when you saw that price? Let's make sure we're aligned on what 
   this includes."

2. Anchor the value:
   "The way I've priced this is based on [value the buyer mentioned — time 
   saved, cost avoided]. Does [outcome] still feel like the right priority 
   for your team this quarter?"

3. Offer a smaller entry:
   "If the full scope is more than you're ready to commit to right now, 
   we could start with a smaller pilot scope at $[lower amount]. That gives 
   you a lower-risk way to evaluate the results before the full engagement."

---

## Objection: "We can get this done internally / with another tool / for less."

This objection reveals that the buyer sees alternatives. It is not necessarily a price objection.

Response options:

1. Acknowledge and explore:
   "That's a reasonable option. What's made you consider bringing someone 
   outside in the first place? What would internal delivery require from your team?"

2. Clarify differentiation:
   "The difference with what I offer is [specific reason — speed, specific 
   expertise, accountability, proven process]. Is that difference relevant 
   to your situation?"

3. Accept gracefully if the fit is not right:
   "If you have a solid internal option, that may be the better path. If you 
   want a second opinion on what internal delivery would realistically require, 
   I'm happy to share what I've seen."

---

## Objection: "Can you do it for less?"

Response options:

1. Reduce scope:
   "I can adjust the price by reducing the scope. What could we remove from 
   this engagement and still have it deliver the outcome you need?"

2. Hold the price:
   "The price reflects the scope and the quality I deliver. I don't want to 
   reduce the price and then deliver something that doesn't meet your 
   expectations. If the budget genuinely doesn't work, let's talk about 
   a smaller starting point."

3. Offer alternative payment structure:
   "I can't adjust the total, but I could offer different payment timing 
   if that helps. Would monthly payments over [X months] make this work better?"

---

## Objection: "I need to think about it / talk to [someone]."

This often means: "I'm not convinced enough yet to say yes."

Response options:

1. Understand the hesitation:
   "Of course. What are the main things you want to think through? Is there 
   any information that would help make the decision clearer?"

2. Clarify the next step:
   "Who else will be involved in the decision? I'm happy to speak with them 
   directly or send materials that address their questions."

3. Set a follow-up:
   "When do you expect to have a clearer sense? I'll follow up on [date]. 
   Does that work?"

---

## Objection: "I had a bad experience with an AI vendor before."

Response options:

1. Acknowledge the experience:
   "That's worth knowing about. What happened? Understanding it helps me 
   tell you whether my approach would be different."

2. Differentiate honestly:
   "The issues you described are common when [cause]. The way I handle that 
   is [specific process]. I'm happy to walk you through exactly how delivery 
   works so you can evaluate whether the same risk applies here."

---

## When to walk away

Not every objection should be resolved. Walk away from a prospect when:
- The budget is genuinely below the cost floor
- The buyer's expectations cannot be met at any price
- The buyer wants a guarantee you cannot honestly make
- The relationship dynamics suggest the engagement would be painful

Turning down poor-fit clients protects your reputation and creates capacity for good-fit clients.
```
