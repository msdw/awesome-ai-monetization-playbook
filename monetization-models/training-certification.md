# Training and Certification

## Overview

Training and certification models sell structured learning experiences — workshops, courses, cohort programs, or certification programs — that teach AI skills, workflows, or implementation practices to individuals or teams.

This model is appropriate for practitioners who have real implementation experience and can transfer practical knowledge. It is not a shortcut for people who have not yet built and delivered AI systems for real clients.

AI training can be delivered synchronously (live workshops, cohort programs) or asynchronously (recorded courses, self-paced programs). Corporate training is often delivered as on-site or virtual half-day or full-day sessions for teams.

Certification programs attach a credential to completion, which adds perceived value but requires a credible assessment process to be meaningful.

## Best For

- AI practitioners teaching practical implementation skills to developers or operators
- AI consultants selling corporate training programs to enterprise teams
- AI tools companies training their users to get more value from the product
- AI educators building courses for freelancers, marketers, or operations professionals
- Companies building internal AI capability through structured cohort programs

## Pricing Structure

Typical observed ranges for AI training:

- Self-paced online course: $97–$497 per student
- Live workshop (public, individual): $200–$1,000 per seat
- Corporate half-day workshop: $2,000–$8,000 per session
- Corporate full-day training: $4,000–$15,000 per session
- Multi-week cohort program: $500–$3,000 per participant
- Certification program: $300–$2,500 per candidate

Corporate training prices reflect group delivery to 10–30 participants, making the per-person cost much lower than the total fee suggests.

Pricing is a hypothesis. These ranges are observed from public training and workshop pricing in the AI and technology learning market.

## Pros & Cons

**Pros**
- Content created once can be sold many times (recorded courses)
- High leverage: teaching a group simultaneously multiplies impact per hour
- Training builds credibility and relationships that convert to consulting clients
- Certifications can generate recurring revenue through renewal or advanced levels

**Cons**
- Credibility and track record are prerequisites — buyers want to learn from practitioners
- Training markets commoditize quickly as content proliferates
- Live delivery is time-intensive and does not scale without hiring additional trainers
- Corporate training sales cycles can be long (procurement, HR, scheduling)

## Real-World Examples

- AI practitioners selling workshops on LinkedIn to marketing and operations teams
- Consulting firms offering AI adoption training programs bundled with implementation services
- AI platform companies (LangChain, Hugging Face) offering developer training as a product line
- Independent educators building AI courses on Gumroad, Maven, or Teachable

These are common patterns in the training and education market. They do not represent specific endorsed individuals or companies.

## How to Implement

1. Choose a specific audience and skill gap — narrow beats broad for training products
2. Build a curriculum based on real problems you have solved, not theoretical knowledge
3. Run the first cohort live before recording — live delivery reveals what learners actually need
4. Gather feedback from every participant and iterate the curriculum before scaling
5. Create proof of outcomes: case studies, student results, testimonials (with permission)
6. Design marketing content (case studies, free samples) that demonstrates your expertise
7. Sell the first corporate engagement through direct outreach before building a sales funnel
8. Decide on certification criteria and assessment before offering a credential

## Common Mistakes

- Teaching AI theory without practical application — buyers want skills they can use immediately
- No proof of expertise — training without demonstrated real-world results is hard to sell
- Over-promising outcomes — training transfers skills, not guaranteed results
- Underpricing corporate training — group delivery to an entire team has high leverage value
- Creating recorded courses before validating with live delivery
- Ignoring the post-training support question — learners often need follow-up access
