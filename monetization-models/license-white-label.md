# License and White-Label

## Overview

Licensing allows another party to use your AI product, workflow, model, or IP under a defined agreement. White-labeling is a specific form of licensing where the licensee sells the product under their own brand.

This model is appropriate when you have built a working AI product or system with proven demand, and when you want to reach new customer segments through partners or resellers rather than direct sales.

Licensing agreements must be reviewed by a lawyer. They define usage rights, restrictions, sublicensing rights, liability, data handling, and termination conditions. Do not use informal licensing agreements for commercial arrangements.

## Best For

- AI chatbot platforms sold to agencies who resell to their clients
- AI workflow systems licensed to complementary SaaS products as embedded features
- Proprietary AI models or fine-tuned models licensed to industry buyers
- AI template systems licensed to consultants for use in client engagements
- AI-powered tools resold through technology partner networks

## Pricing Structure

Common white-label and licensing structures:

- Per-seat license: $50–$500/month per end user seat
- Per-reseller license: $500–$3,000/month flat for unlimited client usage up to a tier
- Revenue share: 10–30% of the reseller's revenue attributable to the licensed product
- One-time perpetual license: $5,000–$50,000 for defined usage rights (less common for AI)
- Enterprise OEM: custom pricing negotiated per partner, typically $12,000–$120,000/year

Pricing depends heavily on the value the licensee generates. White-label pricing should reflect the margin the reseller makes, not only your production cost.

Pricing is a hypothesis. These ranges are observed patterns in white-label software and AI tool markets.

## Pros & Cons

**Pros**
- Access to the licensee's existing customer relationships reduces your direct sales burden
- Revenue can scale through multiple partners simultaneously
- Partners who depend on your product become long-term customers

**Cons**
- Partner dependency — revenue can disappear if a key partner churns
- Limited brand building — customers know the partner's brand, not yours
- Support obligations to partners who may have limited technical skill
- Contract complexity — requires proper legal review

## Real-World Examples

- AI customer support platforms sold white-label to customer service agencies
- AI content generation tools licensed to marketing platforms as embedded features
- AI document analysis systems licensed to legal tech firms under their branding
- Workflow automation tools licensed to consulting firms who bundle them in their service delivery

## How to Implement

1. Validate demand with direct customers before building a partner program
2. Define what exactly is being licensed: the software, the workflow, the model, or all three
3. Prepare proper licensing documentation with legal counsel before approaching partners
4. Create partner onboarding and support materials — partners need to deliver to their clients
5. Decide on white-label branding requirements: can partners fully remove your brand?
6. Build usage reporting so you can invoice partners accurately if using revenue share
7. Set minimum monthly commitments to protect revenue predictability
8. Start with one or two partners to learn before building a formal partner program

## Common Mistakes

- Licensing before having a working, tested product with real customers
- No formal licensing agreement — handshake deals fail when disputes arise
- Underestimating partner support requirements — partners need ongoing help
- No usage tracking — impossible to invoice accurately under revenue share arrangements
- Licensing to too many partners too quickly before the product is stable
- Not defining what the licensee can and cannot do with the product in the agreement
