# Community and Membership

## Overview

A community membership model charges recurring fees for access to a curated group of practitioners, exclusive content, expert access, peer support, or learning resources. In the AI context, this typically means a paid community of people building AI products or services together.

This model is appropriate when the provider can build and moderate a community that delivers sustained peer value, expert access, and exclusive content. It is not appropriate as a primary revenue model for most AI builders — it typically complements other revenue streams.

Communities that succeed do so because of the quality and engagement of members, not because of the technology platform used.

## Best For

- AI practitioners building a community of like-minded builders or founders
- AI educators who want to extend course value with ongoing peer access
- AI consultants building a peer network with exclusive content and expert Q&A
- Niche AI communities focused on specific verticals (AI for healthcare, AI for finance, etc.)
- AI tool companies building a power user community with premium access

## Pricing Structure

Typical observed ranges for AI practitioner communities:

- Entry-level membership: $29–$79/month
- Mid-tier community with expert access: $99–$249/month
- High-value mastermind or small group: $300–$1,500/month
- Annual membership with discount: 2 months free equivalent

Community value is hard to price abstractly. Pricing should be tested against perceived value through pilot cohorts before setting permanent tiers.

## Pros & Cons

**Pros**
- Recurring revenue with relatively low infrastructure cost
- Member network becomes increasingly valuable as the community grows
- Members who engage deeply have high switching cost
- Community can become a lead generation channel for other offers

**Cons**
- Community building requires sustained engagement effort — it is not a low-maintenance model
- Member quality determines member experience — curation is important and ongoing
- Churn accelerates when active discussion declines
- Requires consistent content, events, or expert access to justify ongoing payment

## Real-World Examples

- Indie Hackers community (free with premium elements)
- Maven cohort programs with community components
- Paid Slack or Discord communities for AI practitioners
- Mastermind groups for AI consultants or agency owners

These are common patterns in creator and professional community markets.

## How to Implement

1. Start with a free community to understand what members value before charging
2. Identify the core reason members stay engaged — peer connection, expert access, exclusive content
3. Set a pricing hypothesis and test it with a founder cohort before launching publicly
4. Define community norms, moderation standards, and member expectations clearly
5. Schedule regular programming: weekly calls, expert AMAs, monthly workshops
6. Actively participate in the community yourself — founder engagement drives engagement
7. Create a curation process — not all applicants should be accepted
8. Measure active participation rate, not just member count

## Common Mistakes

- Launching a paid community with no existing free audience to convert
- No active moderation or programming — communities become ghost towns without structure
- Pricing based on desire for revenue rather than member value perception
- No curation — poor member quality damages the experience for everyone
- Treating community as fully automated — requires ongoing human investment
- No clear reason for members to stay month after month
