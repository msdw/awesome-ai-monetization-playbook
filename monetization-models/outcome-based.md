# Outcome-Based Pricing

## Overview

Outcome-based pricing ties fees directly to measurable results rather than time, units, or access. The provider is compensated when the client achieves a defined outcome — leads generated, revenue added, costs reduced, or hours saved.

This model is appropriate when the outcome is measurable, attributable to the AI service, and valuable enough that a percentage of it justifies the provider's cost. It is high-risk for providers because payment depends on results they cannot fully control.

Outcome-based pricing should not be attempted without a strong track record, a clear measurement methodology, and a legally reviewed agreement that defines attribution precisely.

## Best For

- AI lead generation and sales pipeline enrichment services
- AI-powered conversion rate optimization for e-commerce
- AI-driven cost reduction services with measurable baselines
- AI recruitment matching services with placement-based fees
- Performance-based SEO or content services using AI

This model requires mature AI capabilities, proven results with similar clients, and confidence in attribution. It is not appropriate for new services without a track record.

## Pricing Structure

Typical structures:

- Revenue share: 5–20% of directly attributed revenue generated
- Cost savings share: 10–25% of documented cost reduction
- Per placement: fixed fee per outcome (e.g., per qualified lead, per hire)
- Hybrid: small base retainer plus performance bonus above a threshold

All pricing is highly context-dependent. The percentages above are observed in public case studies and standard industry practice descriptions, not universal benchmarks.

## Pros & Cons

**Pros**
- Aligns provider incentives with client success
- Reduces client's upfront financial risk — easier to close deals
- Can generate higher total compensation if outcomes are strong

**Cons**
- Revenue is unpredictable and outside provider's full control
- Attribution disputes are common — defining what counts as an outcome is complex
- Clients may manipulate tracking or dispute results
- Requires detailed contracts reviewed by legal counsel
- Negative cash flow risk if delivery costs are incurred before outcomes materialize

## Real-World Examples

- Recruitment agencies charging a percentage of annual salary for placed candidates
- Affiliate marketing platforms where publishers earn per conversion
- Performance marketing agencies charging per qualified lead or booked meeting
- Revenue operations consultancies with bonuses tied to pipeline growth

Note: outcome-based AI services are relatively uncommon because attribution is technically difficult. Most examples are hybrid arrangements with a base fee plus performance component.

## How to Implement

1. Establish a baseline measurement before starting — document the current state precisely
2. Define the outcome, measurement method, and attribution rules in the contract
3. Agree on a measurement period and reporting cadence with the client
4. Use third-party tracking when possible to avoid attribution disputes
5. Include a floor payment (small base retainer) to cover delivery costs during early phases
6. Build in clear terms for what happens if the AI system underperforms
7. Have legal counsel review the agreement before signing
8. Test the model with one client under a small pilot before scaling it

## Common Mistakes

- Agreeing to outcome-based pricing without defining measurement terms in writing
- No baseline — impossible to prove what changed without measuring the starting point
- Accepting 100% outcome dependency with no base retainer — creates unsustainable cash flow
- Not accounting for factors outside your control (seasonality, client sales team quality)
- Pricing outcomes without modeling delivery costs — possible to lose money despite good results
- Skipping legal review of attribution and payment terms
